#### 1. Euralarm: "10 Years Minimum" to Rewrite Fire Product Standards
Euralarm (European association of fire safety and security industry) has published a strongly critical position on the CPR, stating that the Commission's proposal "does not resolve the ongoing stagnation of the standardisation process and fails to address the market needs." Key concern: all fire product standards and the EN 54 system standard would need complete rewriting to align with Commission requirements, with an estimated timespan of "at best 10 years." This directly affects the FFF DPP timeline — if Euralarm's 10-year estimate proves closer to reality, the ~2030–2033 DPP range may be optimistic.

#### 2. Cyber Resilience Act — "Default" Category for Most Fire Products
Euralarm published a fact sheet (November 2024) clarifying CRA product classification for fire safety manufacturers. Key findings:
- Most electronic fire safety and physical security products fall in the **"default" category** — self-assessment permitted
- Products covered: fire detection control panels, intrusion detection, access control, biometric readers, cloud-based alarm software
- Embedding security components (microcontrollers) does not automatically elevate classification
- Important/Critical categories require notified body involvement but most fire products avoid this
- **CRA reporting obligations from 11 September 2026; full compliance from 11 December 2027**
- EN 62443-4-x and horizontal standards may support conformity demonstrations

This is less burdensome than initially assessed — self-assessment for most products means no additional notified body costs for CRA. However, CRA adds cybersecurity essential requirements to product design regardless of assessment route.

#### 3. EN 12101-2 Citation Issue — 2003 vs 2017 Versions Clarified
Extended research reveals the source of the EN 12101-2 persistent citation issue:
- **EN 12101-2:2003** is the harmonised version (OJ-cited, AVCP System 1)
- **EN 12101-2:2017** was published but is **NOT harmonised** — never cited in the OJ
- Manufacturers must still use the 2003 version for CE marking
- This may explain why EN 12101-2 is absent from the tracker's standards[] array — the data compilation may have excluded it because the current published version (2017) is not harmonised, even though the 2003 version remains the legal reference

#### 4. EN 54 Citation Backlog — No Progress
No evidence found of any resolution to the EN 54 citation backlog. The 6+ uncited but technically sound EN 54 parts remain in limbo. No implementing decisions in 2025 or 2026 address EN 54 citations. The new CPR Art. 5(8) citation-by-implementing-act mechanism exists but has not been applied to fire detection standards.

#### 5. EN 50130-4 EMC Revision Confirmed Active
Euralarm confirms the revision of EN 50130-4 (EMC standard for fire and security systems) is actively impacting the fire and security industry. This is a supporting standard, not a harmonised product standard, but its revision affects conformity assessment for all EN 54-series products.

## Supplementary Wide-Scope Research

### S1. PFAS Ban on Firefighting Foams — Regulation (EU) 2025/1988

The European Commission adopted Regulation (EU) 2025/1988 on 2 October 2025, amending REACH Annex XVII to restrict PFAS ("forever chemicals") in firefighting foams. Key deadlines: portable fire extinguishers cannot be filled with PFAS foam from 23 October 2026; alcohol-resistant foams banned from 23 April 2027; full ban on placing on market/use from 23 October 2030; existing PFAS-containing extinguishers may be used until 31 December 2030. Fluorine-free alternatives must meet ≤50 mg/L PFAS concentration (from equipment cleaned with BAT). This directly impacts FFF products covered by the EN 12094 series (gas extinguishing) and EN 12259 series (sprinkler/water spray) — foam agent compatibility will need to be reassessed. Creates a cross-regulatory burden: CPR harmonised standards (product performance) + REACH (chemical restriction) + future DPP (chemical content declaration).

**Source**: DNV PFOS prohibition (https://www.dnv.com/news/2025/pfos-prohibited-in-fire-extinguishing-media-from-1-january-2026/); E.P.Fire PFAS ban guide (https://epfire.de/en/pfas-ban-firefighting-foam); UL Solutions PFAS restriction (https://www.ul.com/news/new-eu-restriction-pfas-firefighting-foams); CWS fire safety (https://www.cws.com/en/fire-safety/news/pfas-ban-fire-extinguishers-current-status); CIRS Group EU PFAS ban (https://www.cirs-group.com/en/chemicals/eu-adopts-ban-on-pfas-in-firefighting-foam-reach-regulation-amendment-to-take-full-effect-by-2030)

### S2. Zero Fire Product EPDs Found — Industry EPD Readiness Appears Non-Existent

Extensive searching found no Environmental Product Declarations (EPDs) for fire detection, fire alarm, sprinkler, or gas extinguishing products under EN 15804. Unlike cement (thousands of EPDs), concrete (hundreds), steel (dozens), or even chimneys (Schiedel has one), the fire safety product sector appears to have zero EPD infrastructure. This is significant because the new CPR 2024/3110 mandates GWP declarations for DPP. Fire products are manufactured goods with complex electronics, metals, plastics, and chemicals — GWP data will be harder to generate than for single-material construction products. The fire safety industry will need to build EPD capability from scratch.

**Source**: EPD International search (https://www.environdec.com/services/what-is-epd) — no fire product EPDs found; BRE Group EN 15804 services (https://bregroup.com/services/standards/environmental/en-15804-environmental-product-declaration-epd) — no fire sector clients mentioned

### S3. IoT/Smart Building Integration — Wireless Fire Detection Market $1.6B→$3.1B by 2035

The wireless fire detection systems market is projected to grow from $1.62 billion (2025) to $3.1 billion (2035), driven by smart building adoption and IoT integration. Modern fire systems integrate alarms with HVAC (automatic shutdown in fire zones), access control, CCTV, and building management systems. Mesh-networked sensors provide redundancy, and 5G-compatible wireless fire safety networks are emerging. This creates a DPP complexity issue: networked fire products are systems-of-components, not standalone products. EN 54 was designed for discrete product certification, but IoT-integrated fire systems blur the product/system boundary. The Cyber Resilience Act (CRA) adds a third regulatory layer — CRA (cybersecurity) + CPR (construction performance) + potentially ESPR (electronics repairability) all applying to the same networked fire product.

**Source**: FMI wireless fire detection market report (https://www.fmiblog.com/2025/06/19/wireless-fire-detection-systems-market-to-reach-usd-3-1-billion-by-2035-driven-by-smart-building-adoption-and-iot-integration/); ScienceDirect IoT fire safety review (https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2542660523001269); MDPI Industry 4.0 fire management (https://www.mdpi.com/2571-6255/7/10/335)

### S4. EN 12101-2 Citation — Confirmed 2003 Harmonised, 2017 NOT Harmonised

Multiple UK and EU sources confirm: EN 12101-2:2003 is the harmonised version under CPR for smoke and heat exhaust ventilators (SHEVs), cited in the OJ. EN 12101-2:2017 was published as an update but is NOT harmonised and NOT cited in the OJ. Manufacturers must still use the 2003 version for CE marking. This "phantom standard" situation (current published version ≠ harmonised version) is the same pattern seen in other families (e.g., EN 459-1:2015 published but EN 459-1:2010 harmonised for CEM). It confirms the extended addendum finding and explains why EN 12101-2 may have been deliberately excluded from the tracker: the compilation may have captured only the 2017 version and excluded it as non-harmonised.

**Source**: Smoke Control Association SHEV guidance (https://www.smokecontrol.org.uk/client/files/Guidance_on_Smoke_and_Heat_Exhaust_Ventilators__EN12101-2_2003_v7_1.pdf); DIN Media EN 12101-2:2017 (https://www.dinmedia.de/en/standard/din-en-12101-2/234550929); WindowMaster FPC guide (https://www.windowmaster.com/media/osdkbscs/fpc-smoke-guide-eng-v3b.pdf)

### S6. James Elliott Ruling — Broader Standardisation Crisis Context

The James Elliott judgment (CJEU C-613/14, 2016) and the subsequent Global Garden judgment (EGC, 2017) together created the standardisation crisis that caused the EN 54 citation backlog. The Commission responded by pledging to "eliminate the remaining backlog as rapidly as possible" and reviewing internal decision-making processes. However, as of March 2026, EN 54 parts remain uncited. The new CPR 2024/3110 Art. 5(8) mechanism (citation by implementing act rather than OJ C-series publication) was specifically designed to address this, but has not yet been applied to fire detection standards.

**Source**: Johner Institute EU case law impact (https://www.johner-institute.com/articles/regulatory-affairs/and-more/james-elliott-and-global-garden-impact-of-eu-case-law-on-standard-harmonization/); EUR-Lex case C-613/14 (https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:62014CA0613)
