### S2. HUESKER and Naue Publish EN 15804 EPDs — Geogrid First EPD a Milestone for the Industry

HUESKER Synthetic GmbH publishes EN 15804 EPDs for Fortrac® T geogrid (verified by Kiwa, modules A1-A3). Naue publishes EPDs for Secugrid® geogrids (primarily used for reinforcement of unbound aggregate layers in road pavements). Naue's EPD download page covers multiple product lines. The HUESKER Fortrac geogrid EPD was highlighted by Geosynthetica as the first EPD for a geogrid product — a milestone for the geosynthetics industry. EPD coverage appears limited to market leaders and reinforcement products (geogrids); EPDs for geotextiles (filtration/separation), geomembranes (barrier), and geocomposites (drainage) were not prominently found. For DPP: the geosynthetics industry is early in its EPD journey, with reinforcement products leading but the broader portfolio lagging.

### S3. Geosynthetics Recycling Emerging — Netherlands 500,000 m² Recycled, 100% Recycled PP Geogrids Developed

Construction sector produces 850+ million tonnes waste annually in Europe, with geosynthetics significantly underrepresented in circular economy strategies. Key developments: Netherlands project successfully recycled 500,000 m² of geotextiles from temporary construction roads — presented at EuroGeo8 (2025) as a collaborative approach to enhance circularity. New composite geosynthetics manufactured from 100% upcycled polypropylene (FHWA-documented). 3D-printed model geogrids from recycled plastics being evaluated. PET and PP are most commonly recycled polymers for geosynthetics — PET for high tensile strength geogrids, PP for flexible geotextiles. However, economic and technical barriers remain: high soil contamination content in recovered geotextiles, cost disparities with virgin material. CE marking of recycled polymers in geosynthetics being explored (TCS Geotechnics).

**Source**: EuroGeo8 recycling paper (https://www.e3s-conferences.org/articles/e3sconf/pdf/2025/44/e3sconf_eurogeo82025_01004.pdf); FHWA upcycling (https://highways.dot.gov/public-roads/winter-2025/08); IGS spotlight on sustainability (https://www.geosyntheticssociety.org/spotlight-on-sustainable-initiatives-in-geosynthetics/); TCS CE marking recycled (https://www.tcs-geotechnics.co.uk/ce-marking-of-recycled-polymers-in-geosynthetics-tcs-geotechnics/)

### S5. IGS Defends Geosynthetics on Microplastics — 65% Lower Carbon Footprint Than Alternatives

The International Geosynthetics Society (IGS) has formally responded to the European Commission on microplastics concerns, arguing that engineered geosynthetics "do not normally degrade" unlike consumer plastics. IGS position: vehicle tyres (55%), pellets (28%), and clothing textiles (8%) account for majority of marine microplastics — not engineered geosynthetics. IGS advocates science-based evidence over blanket restrictions. Key sustainability claims: geosynthetics carbon footprint typically 65% lower than alternative construction materials; they extend road service life, reduce aggregate use, conserve water, and control erosion. For DPP: the microplastics debate could introduce additional environmental reporting requirements for geosynthetics beyond current CE marking scope — particularly for exposed geotextiles in erosion control and green infrastructure.

**Source**: IGS sustainability (https://www.geosyntheticssociety.org/sustainability/); Nonwovens News EU microplastics (https://www.nonwovensnews.com/manufacturing-news/15928-eu-investigating-release-of-microplastics-from-geotextiles); IGS plastic waste protection (https://www.geosyntheticssociety.org/did-you-know-geosynthetics-protect-the-environment-from-plastic-waste/)

### S6. HDPE Geomembranes Critical for PFAS Containment — EU PFAS Ban Creates Dual Regulatory Pressure

HDPE geomembranes are critical infrastructure for containing PFAS-laden leachate in landfills — intact membranes can contain PFAS for 30+ years (permeability <10⁻¹⁴ cm/s). Research shows PFAS diffusion through HDPE barely detected up to 985 days. However, the February 2023 ECHA proposal to ban PFAS (including process aids) creates dual regulatory pressure: some geomembrane manufacturing processes use PFAS-containing processing aids, and manufacturers are increasingly developing PFAS-free products. Short-chain PFAS (PFBA, PFBS) mobility remains a research concern. For DPP: geomembranes for waste containment (EN 13257, EN 13265) may need to declare both their PFAS containment performance AND their own PFAS-free manufacturing status — an unusual dual-direction data requirement.

**Source**: ScienceDirect PFAS permeation (https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0269749124019511); EPI geomembrane PFAS containment (https://www.geomembrane.com/about/blog/pfas-containment-why-liners-are-now-more-important-than-ever.html); ECHA PFAS restriction proposal (Feb 2023)
